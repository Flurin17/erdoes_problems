# Elementary constant-gap search: grids, matchings, and delayed support

## Objective and outcome

Let
\[
B_f(N)=\{n\le N:f(n)=1\},\qquad E_f(N)=[1,N]\setminus B_f(N),
\]
where \(f:\mathbb N\to\mathbb Q\) is completely additive. The desired
finite negative result would be an absolute \(c>0\) such that
\[
|E_f(N)|\ge cN.                                                   \tag{1}
\]

This note does **not** prove (1) in full. It gives the following rigorous
pieces.

1. It proves (1), with an absolute constant, for every canonical moving
   threshold \(f_y(p)=\mathbf 1_{p>y}\).
2. Using the standard exact-atom theorem for additive functions, it proves
   (1) for arbitrary nonzero prime colours on a full delayed tail.
3. It gives the exact active-prime/zero-prime factorization formula and
   reduces the general case to a boundary-sensitive bounded-budget sieve
   lemma.
4. It identifies why multiplier matchings and terminal valuation grids stop
   at a long zero-coloured cofactor spine.

No product-measure approximation is used in the threshold proof.

## 1. Exact multiplier and factorization lemmas

Call a prime \(p\) **active** when \(f(p)\ne0\), and **zero** otherwise.

### Lemma 1 (one-prime chains)

If \(p\) is active, then
\[
|E_f(N)|\ge \left\lfloor\frac Np\right\rfloor .                    \tag{2}
\]

#### Proof

Partition \([1,N]\) into its \(p\)-adic chains
\[
\{a,ap,\ldots,ap^r\},\qquad p\nmid a.
\]
Along a chain the \(f\)-values form an arithmetic progression with nonzero
difference \(f(p)\), so the value \(1\) occurs at most once. The number of
chains is \(N-\lfloor N/p\rfloor\); hence at least
\(\lfloor N/p\rfloor\) vertices are outside the level. \(\square\)

For one multiplier this is sharp in order. If the least active prime tends
to infinity, (2) is \(o(N)\). Summing (2) over primes is invalid: the
deleted representatives from many chains can be the same small integers.

### Lemma 2 (exact active/zero core decomposition)

Let \(\mathcal A\) be the multiplicative monoid generated by the active
primes and \(\mathcal Z\) the monoid generated by the zero primes. Put
\[
Z(x)=|\mathcal Z\cap[1,x]|.
\]
Then every integer has a unique factorization \(n=az\), with
\(a\in\mathcal A\), \(z\in\mathcal Z\), and
\[
\begin{aligned}
N&=\sum_{a\in\mathcal A}Z(N/a),\\
|B_f(N)|&=\sum_{\substack{a\in\mathcal A\\f(a)=1}}Z(N/a).          \tag{3}
\end{aligned}
\]
Here terms with \(a>N\) are understood to be zero.

#### Proof

Put the full prime power \(p^{v_p(n)}\) in \(a\) or \(z\) according as \(p\)
is active or zero. This is unique, \(f(z)=0\), and \(f(n)=f(a)\). Summing
over the active part gives both identities. \(\square\)

Formula (3) is the finite boundary obstruction in its cleanest form. It is
not an independent geometric model: the weight of an active part \(a\) is
the truncated zero-core count \(Z(N/a)\).

## 2. A universal gap for the canonical threshold

For \(1\le y\le N\), define
\[
f_y(p)=\mathbf 1_{p>y}
\]
on primes and extend it completely additively. Thus
\[
f_y(n)=\Omega_{>y}(n)
 :=\sum_{\substack{p>y\\p^e\parallel n}}e,                         \tag{4}
\]
and the level \(f_y=1\) consists of integers having exactly one prime factor
above \(y\), counted with multiplicity.

### Proposition 3 (threshold profiles have an absolute gap)

There is an absolute \(c_{\rm th}>0\) such that, uniformly for every moving
choice \(y=y(N)\),
\[
|\{n\le N:f_y(n)=1\}|\le(1-c_{\rm th}+o(1))N.                     \tag{5}
\]
For example, one may take
\[
c_{\rm th}=\min\left(\frac{\rho(8)}2,
                     \frac{(\log2)^2}{400}\right),                \tag{6}
\]
where \(\rho\) is the Dickman function.

#### Proof

First suppose \(y>N^{1/8}\). Every \(N^{1/8}\)-smooth integer has value
zero, and is therefore outside the level. The standard fixed-\(u\) Dickman
asymptotic gives
\[
\Psi(N,N^{1/8})=(\rho(8)+o(1))N.                                  \tag{7}
\]

Now suppose \(y\le N^{1/8}\), and let
\[
I=(N^{1/8},N^{1/4}]\cap\mathcal P.
\]
For every two distinct \(p,q\in I\) and every \(k\le N/(pq)\), the integer
\(pqk\) has at least two prime factors above \(y\), so it is outside the
level. Count triples with \(p<q\):
\[
T=\sum_{\substack{p<q\\p,q\in I}}
   \left\lfloor\frac N{pq}\right\rfloor .                          \tag{8}
\]
Since \(pq\le N^{1/2}\), for all large \(N\),
\[
T\ge\frac N2\sum_{\substack{p<q\\p,q\in I}}\frac1{pq}.             \tag{9}
\]
The prime number theorem and partial summation give
\[
\sum_{p\in I}\frac1p=\log2+o(1),\qquad
\sum_{p\in I}\frac1{p^2}=o(1),                                   \tag{10}
\]
so the sum in (9) is \((\log2)^2/2+o(1)\).

An integer at most \(N\) has at most seven distinct prime factors from
\(I\), because every such prime is strictly larger than \(N^{1/8}\).
Consequently it is represented in (8) by at most
\(\binom72=21\) unordered pairs. The number of distinct exceptional
integers in (8) is therefore at least
\[
\frac{T}{21}\ge
\left(\frac{(\log2)^2}{100}+o(1)\right)N,                          \tag{11}
\]
where the weaker denominator \(100\) leaves ample endpoint slack.
Equations (7) and (11) prove (5)--(6). \(\square\)

This proof is insensitive to the threshold scale. Above \(N^{1/8}\), a
fixed positive Dickman mass lies on the zero level. Below \(N^{1/8}\), a
bounded-overlap family of two-large-prime multiples lies on levels at least
two.

## 3. Delayed support with arbitrary colours

If merely
\[
f(p)=0\qquad(p\le y),                                             \tag{12}
\]
then every \(y\)-smooth integer is outside the nonzero level. Hence, for
each fixed \(u\),
\[
y\ge N^{1/u}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
|E_f(N)|\ge(\rho(u)+o(1))N.                                      \tag{13}
\]
This is not uniform when \(u\to\infty\).

The second half of Proposition 3 does not extend verbatim to arbitrary
nonzero weights above \(y\): the products \(pqk\) may have
\(f(p)+f(q)+f(k)=1\). For example, a zero-coloured cofactor together with
two prime weights \(1/2\) lies on the target level.

There is nevertheless a complete statement if there are no holes in the
active tail. The only non-elementary input is the exact-atom theorem stated
in (17).

### Proposition 4 (arbitrary colours on a full delayed tail)

Suppose that, for some \(y=y(N)\),
\[
f(p)=0\quad(p\le y),\qquad f(p)\ne0\quad(y<p\le N).                \tag{14}
\]
Then there is an absolute \(c_{\rm tail}>0\), independent of the values and
signs of the nonzero \(f(p)\), such that
\[
|E_f(N)|\ge(c_{\rm tail}+o(1))N.                                 \tag{15}
\]

#### Proof

Let \(C\) be the absolute constant in (17). Fix a sufficiently large
absolute \(U\) such that
\[
\frac{C}{\sqrt{1+\log(U/2)}}<\frac12.                             \tag{16}
\]
If \(y\ge N^{1/U}\), every \(N^{1/U}\)-smooth integer has value zero, so
\[
|E_f(N)|\ge(\rho(U)+o(1))N.
\]
If \(y<N^{1/U}\), every prime in
\((N^{1/U},N^{1/2}]\) is active. The prime number theorem and partial
summation give reciprocal mass \(\log(U/2)+o(1)\) in this interval.
Equation (17) therefore bounds the density of the level \(1\) by
\(1/2+o(1)\). Thus one can take
\[
c_{\rm tail}=\min(\rho(U)/2,1/3)>0.
\]
\(\square\)

## 4. The harmonic-mass dichotomy

Define the active reciprocal mass
\[
S_f(N)=\sum_{\substack{p\le N\\f(p)\ne0}}\frac1p.
\]

A standard exact-atom theorem of Halász states that, for a real additive
function \(g\),
\[
\frac1N\max_t|\{n\le N:g(n)=t\}|
\le \frac{C}
{\sqrt{1+\sum_{p\le N,\,g(p)\ne0}1/p}},                           \tag{17}
\]
with an absolute \(C\). This exact-value formulation is being invoked from
standard mathematical memory; no numerical value of \(C\) is asserted.
It is not the interval-concentration theorem, whose statement has an
additional logarithmic-drift term. Equation (17) proves an absolute gap
whenever \(S_f(N)\) exceeds a sufficiently large absolute constant.

The remaining case would follow from the following boundary-sensitive
sieve statement.

### Lemma 5 (bounded-budget zero kernel)

For every \(S_0<\infty\), there is a \(c(S_0)>0\) such that, uniformly
for every \(N\) and every moving prime set
\(\mathcal P_N\subseteq\{p:p\le N\}\) satisfying
\[
\sum_{p\in\mathcal P_N}\frac1p\le S_0,                            \tag{18}
\]
one has
\[
\left|\{n\le N:p\nmid n\text{ for every }p\in\mathcal P_N\}\right|
\ge c(S_0)N.                                                     \tag{19}
\]

Taking \(\mathcal P_N\) to be the active primes makes the set in (19) a
subset of \(f^{-1}(0)\), hence of \(E_f(N)\). Choose \(S_0\) so large that
(17) is at most \(1/2\) above \(S_0\). Equations (17) and (19) would prove
(1).

This reduction is exact. It also calibrates (19). For
\[
\mathcal P_N=\{p>N^{1/u}\},
\]
the left side of (19) is \((\rho(u)+o(1))N\), while the reciprocal budget
is \(\log u+o(1)\). Thus \(c(S_0)\) may be as small as roughly
\(\rho(e^{S_0})\); an independent-prime lower bound of order \(e^{-S_0}\)
is false at the finite size boundary.

I do not prove (19) here. Ordinary inclusion-exclusion has a genuine
obstruction: intersections of large-prime divisibility events disappear
when the prime product exceeds \(N\), exactly the effect responsible for
the Dickman rather than Euler-product answer. A proof needs a uniform
boundary sieve, a largest-prime recursion, or a quantitative compactness
argument for the scale-invariant prime-factor limit.

## 5. Why multiplier grids stop at the zero spine

For any active multiplier \(q\), the pairs \(k,qk\), \(k\le N/q\), cannot
both lie in \(B_f(N)\). With one prime this gives Lemma 1. For many large
multipliers, however, all lower endpoints lie in a short prefix. A delayed
threshold makes the failure exact:
\[
f(p)=0\ (p\le\sqrt N),\qquad f(p)=1\ (p>\sqrt N).                 \tag{20}
\]
Every lower endpoint \(k\le N/p<\sqrt N\) of a large-prime edge has value
zero, while \(pk\) has value one. All multiplier constraints are paid by
the same zero-coloured prefix, although the actual complement has Dickman
size
\[
(1-\log2+o(1))N.                                                 \tag{21}
\]

The same obstruction appears in prime--cofactor rectangles. After a colour
cut, the cofactor target can become zero and remain zero through every
descendant rectangle. Terminal multiplicative energy then counts only a
single prefix product set. A linear argument must count the whole
zero-prime kernel, as in (19), or invoke anti-concentration from large
active reciprocal mass, as in (17).

## 6. Falsification tests

Any proposed closure should survive all of the following.

1. **Square-root threshold.** Model (20) has level-one density
   \(\log2+o(1)\), not the independent-geometric value \((\log2)/2\).
2. **Deep delayed support.** If \(y=N^{1/u_N}\) with \(u_N\to\infty\), the
   smooth-number lower bound \(\rho(u_N)N\) is not uniformly linear.
3. **Tiny weights.** Arbitrarily small but nonzero rational weights remain
   active for exact equality; magnitude-based variance estimates miss them.
4. **Signed weights.** Two active prime weights can cancel, so merely having
   two active prime divisors does not force an exception.
5. **Endpoint overlap.** Summing (2) over primes is invalid without a
   multiplicity ledger; delayed support makes the overlap maximal.

## Current bottleneck

The canonical threshold case and the full delayed-tail case are closed.
For arbitrary sparse or perforated active support, the weakest clean
missing statement is the bounded-budget kernel lemma (19). Together with
the standard exact-atom theorem (17), it would prove the desired absolute
finite density gap. Without (19), valuation-grid and disjoint-multiplier
arguments alone do not exclude a long all-zero cofactor spine.
